# Growth — Referrals & Network Effects

The single cheapest user-acquisition channel is **other users**. A well-designed referral loop turns every active user into a recruiter — and in MiniPay's audience (WhatsApp-heavy, group-chat-driven, high trust in personal recommendations), it's the channel with the highest realistic ROI for a solo founder.

This file covers how to design a referral program that actually moves the needle: dual-sided incentives, leaderboards that don't demotivate the long tail, anti-sybil that doesn't kill conversion, and on-chain tracking patterns.

---

## 1. Why referrals work especially well for Celo/MiniPay apps

Three structural reasons referrals out-perform paid acquisition for Celo projects:

1. **The target user lives in group chats.** WhatsApp groups (Africa), Telegram channels (LatAm crypto), Discord/Farcaster (English crypto-native) — all of these reward shareable, screenshot-friendly content. A referral link sent in a group chat reaches 10-100 people at once at zero cost.
2. **Trust is the bottleneck, not awareness.** In emerging markets, users have been burned by scam apps. A friend's recommendation closes the trust gap that an ad never can.
3. **You can pay rewards in USDT on-chain, with sub-cent transaction costs.** Most web2 referral programs choke on the cost of payouts. On Celo, sending a $0.50 referral reward costs the founder less than $0.001 in network fees (and zero in payment processing).

## 2. The dual-sided incentive principle (non-negotiable)

The biggest mistake builders make is rewarding *only* the inviter. This kills conversion at the invitee step — the new user has no reason to follow through.

**Both sides must win.** The asymmetry of rewards depends on your business model, but neither side can be zero.

| Side | What they get | Why |
|------|---------------|-----|
| **Inviter** | Recurring upside (a % of invitee's future activity), USDT bonus, leaderboard status | Motivates ongoing recruitment, not a one-shot share |
| **Invitee** | A welcome bonus, fee waiver, premium feature unlock, head-start in a leaderboard | Removes friction at signup — the moment you have the most drop-off |

Examples of dual-sided splits that work:
- "Both of you get $1 USDT credit when your friend makes their first deposit"
- "Your friend gets 0% fees for their first month; you get a 10% revenue share on their fees forever after"
- "Your friend skips the waitlist; you move up 10 spots in the leaderboard for each successful referral"

## 3. The 4 referral pattern archetypes (pick one to start)

You can mix patterns over time, but launch with **one** so users can explain it to a friend in one sentence.

### Pattern A — Flat USDT bounty (the savings/payments default)

Both sides get a fixed USDT amount when a referral converts (defined as the invitee taking the primary action — first deposit, first transaction, etc.).

- **Best for**: payment apps, savings apps, anything with a clear "first action"
- **Typical amount**: $1-5 USDT per side
- **Pros**: simple to explain, simple to budget, easy to measure ROI
- **Cons**: doesn't compound; once the bounty is paid, the inviter has no further reason to recruit

### Pattern B — Revenue share (the long-term default)

The inviter earns a % of every future fee/commission generated by the invitee, forever (or for a bounded period like 12 months).

- **Best for**: high-LTV apps (subscriptions, recurring transactions, trading platforms)
- **Typical amount**: 10-30% revenue share, often tapered over time
- **Pros**: aligned with retention — inviters care about who they refer
- **Cons**: complex to explain ("you get 15% of my friend's fees for a year" is harder than "$2"), harder to budget, harder to communicate the upside

### Pattern C — Fee waiver / feature unlock (the freemium default)

The invitee gets a free month of premium, or a fee-free first N transactions. The inviter gets a similar perk for each successful referral.

- **Best for**: subscription apps, apps with clear free vs paid tiers
- **Typical**: 1 month free per referral, capped at 12 months
- **Pros**: zero cash outflow (waivers cost you nothing if the user wouldn't have paid anyway)
- **Cons**: only works if the paid feature is genuinely valuable; otherwise nobody cares about the unlock

### Pattern D — Leaderboard + season prizes (the gamified default)

Referrals contribute points toward a public leaderboard. Top N each season win prizes (USDT, NFTs, IRL items, status).

- **Best for**: community-driven apps, games, social products, anything with a fanbase
- **Typical**: weekly/monthly seasons, top 10-50 win, $50-500 USDT prizes
- **Pros**: creates the most viral spikes (top users *evangelize* hard for the prize)
- **Cons**: demotivates the long tail (whoever isn't in the top 50 stops trying), seasonal cliff risk (engagement drops between seasons)

**Strong recommendation for solo founders pre-PMF**: start with **Pattern A (flat USDT bounty)** — fastest to build, cleanest to measure, easiest for users to understand. Add Pattern D (leaderboard) on top once you have ≥500 active users to make the leaderboard credible.

## 4. Leaderboards that don't demotivate the long tail

A naive leaderboard shows the top 10. Everyone outside the top 10 sees they're not winning and gives up. The fix is layered tiers and personal progress.

### Design rules for leaderboards that keep the long tail engaged

1. **Show the user's personal rank** — every viewer sees both the top 10 AND "You are #247 — 5 more referrals to reach #200". Their *trajectory* matters more than their absolute rank.
2. **Tier-based rewards, not top-N-only** — instead of "top 10 win $100," try "top 10 win $100, top 100 win a free month, top 1000 win a badge." This way 90% of active users get *something*, which keeps them in the game.
3. **Reset on a cadence** — weekly or monthly seasons mean newcomers always have a fresh chance. A leaderboard that compounds since launch is impossible to climb if you join late.
4. **Show absolute numbers, not just rank** — "Maria invited 47 friends this week" is more inspiring than "Maria is #3". Numbers are copyable; ranks are zero-sum.
5. **Recognize categories, not just totals** — "Most invites this week," "Most invites from a new country," "Most invites who actually deposited," "Highest deposit volume from invitees." Multiple winners across categories = more people seeing their name.

## 5. On-chain referral tracking patterns

There are 3 standard patterns. Pick one based on how on-chain-native your app is.

### Pattern 1 — Smart contract referrer field (cleanest, most credible)

Store the referrer's address in the contract when a user takes their first action. All subsequent actions emit events with the original referrer, allowing payouts to be computed deterministically.

```solidity
mapping(address => address) public referrerOf;  // user → who referred them

function depositWithReferrer(uint256 amount, address referrer) external {
    if (referrerOf[msg.sender] == address(0) && referrer != msg.sender) {
        referrerOf[msg.sender] = referrer;
        emit ReferralRegistered(msg.sender, referrer);
    }
    // ... deposit logic
    if (referrerOf[msg.sender] != address(0)) {
        uint256 referralReward = amount * REFERRAL_BPS / 10_000;
        IERC20(usdt).transfer(referrerOf[msg.sender], referralReward);
        emit ReferralRewardPaid(referrerOf[msg.sender], msg.sender, referralReward);
    }
}
```

**Pros**: trust-minimized, auditable, payouts can be claimed by the referrer directly even if your frontend is down.
**Cons**: requires the user's first-action transaction to include the referrer (need to pass it through the UI cleanly).

### Pattern 2 — Off-chain attribution → on-chain payout

The frontend uses a referral code (`?ref=ABCD`) in the URL. Your backend matches the code to the inviter address. Payouts are batched on-chain weekly.

**Pros**: works for users who don't see the referrer field (e.g. mobile WebView quirks), allows non-on-chain referral sources (Twitter, WhatsApp share links).
**Cons**: requires you to trust the backend's attribution — if your team is questioned about referral fraud, you can't prove the chain of attribution like Pattern 1.

### Pattern 3 — Phone-number referrals via ODIS (MiniPay-native)

Use ODIS phone-number → address mapping (see `odis-socialconnect.md`) to let users refer a contact by phone number. When the contact installs the app, the referrer is automatically credited.

**Pros**: most natural UX for MiniPay users — they refer the same way they share anything else (by contact). No copy-paste of cryptic codes.
**Cons**: requires ODIS integration; only works once both users have phone-verified.

## 6. Anti-sybil — don't pay for fake referrals

The biggest risk of an open referral program: one person creates 100 wallets, refers themselves, and drains your treasury. Mitigations, ranked by effectiveness vs. friction:

| Mitigation | Effectiveness | Friction added |
|------------|---------------|----------------|
| Cap rewards per referrer per period (e.g. max $20/week) | Medium | None |
| Require invitee to take a meaningful action (deposit ≥$X) before reward unlocks | High | Low |
| Hold reward in escrow for 14 days, release only if invitee is still active | High | Medium |
| Require phone verification via ODIS | High | Medium (excludes users without phones) |
| Require ZK personhood proof via **Self.xyz** | Very high | High (10-20% drop-off) |
| Manual review of suspicious patterns (one wallet referring 50+ in a day) | Medium | High operational cost |

**Recommended starting stack**: cap rewards per referrer per week + require invitee to take a meaningful first action + hold rewards in 7-day escrow. This blocks 90%+ of sybil attempts without adding friction for real users.

If you're paying meaningful USDT (>$5 per referral) or your app explicitly markets the referral program ("earn $$ by inviting friends"), graduate to Self.xyz ZK passport verification — see `minipay-self-zk.md` (ships in a follow-up PR). The 10-20% drop-off is worth it when fraud risk is real money.

## 7. The metrics that matter (viral coefficient ≥ 1.0 is the goal)

Track these in PostHog or your subgraph (see [growth-analytics.md](growth-analytics.md)):

| Metric | Formula | Healthy range |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| **Invites sent per active user** | Total invites sent / active users | 0.5 - 3.0 |
| **Invite conversion rate** | Invitees who converted / invitees reached | 5% - 25% |
| **Viral coefficient (k-factor)** | invites_per_user × conversion_rate | ≥ 1.0 means viral growth; < 1.0 means decay |
| **CAC via referrals** | Total referral payouts / new users from referrals | Should be < CAC via paid ads |
| **Time-to-first-referral** | Days from signup to user's first invite sent | < 7 days = product is sticky |
| **Referrer retention** | % of referrers who refer again within 30 days | ≥ 30% means program is engaging |

A k-factor ≥ 1.0 is the holy grail — every user brings ≥ 1 new user, and growth compounds. Most apps land between 0.2 and 0.7, which means referrals contribute meaningfully but don't replace other channels.

## 8. Prompts

### Prompt: "Design my referral program from scratch"

```
I'm designing a referral program for {project name} — {one-sentence pitch}.

My business model: {pattern from business-model.md §3 — commission /
sale / subscription / agent calls — with my actual rate}.

My current users: {number; their primary action; LTV estimate if you have one}.

Help me design the v1 referral program by answering:

1. WHICH PATTERN — given my business model, which of the 4 archetypes
   (flat bounty / revenue share / fee waiver / leaderboard) fits best?
   What's the trade-off vs. the runner-up?

2. THE DUAL-SIDED SPLIT — exact amounts for inviter and invitee.
   The inviter amount should NOT exceed my LTV (otherwise I'm
   losing money per user). The invitee amount should be enough to
   convert a skeptical first-timer.

3. THE TRIGGER — what action by the invitee unlocks the reward?
   (Signup is too cheap; deposit ≥$X is better; "active for 7 days"
   is even better.)

4. THE ESCROW — should rewards be paid immediately or held in a 7-day
   escrow to defend against churn-and-burn?

5. THE BUDGET CAP — what's the maximum monthly payout? At what point
   does my CAC via referrals exceed my CAC via paid channels?

6. THE ANTI-SYBIL — what's the minimum viable defense for v1?
   (Default: cap per referrer per week + meaningful invitee action.)

7. THE COMMUNICATION — in ONE sentence the user could send via WhatsApp,
   how would they pitch the app + referral incentive to a friend?

Output as a 1-page program spec I could implement this week.
```

### Prompt: "Design a leaderboard that doesn't demotivate"

```
I want a referral leaderboard for {project name}.

I expect to have ~{X} users by launch. Realistic active rate: ~{Y}%.

Help me design it:

1. The tiering — instead of "top 10 win," propose 3-4 tiers with prizes.
   Make sure 80%+ of active users land in a tier that earns *something*
   (badge, status, small reward).

2. The cadence — weekly, monthly, or seasonal? Justify based on my user
   activity frequency.

3. The personal display — what does each user see when they view the
   leaderboard? Show me the layout: top 10 + their personal rank + their
   trajectory + tier they're closest to.

4. The categories — beyond "most referrals," propose 3-4 categories
   that let different user types win (e.g. "most referrals from a new
   country," "highest deposit from an invitee," etc.).

5. The reset — what carries over between seasons and what resets to zero?

6. The MiniPay copy — apply the banned-terms rules (no "Gas", "Crypto",
   raw 0x addresses — use phone/alias for display names).

Output as a leaderboard design spec + the SQL/HogQL queries I'd run
to compute the rankings.
```

### Prompt: "Audit my existing referral program"

```
Here's my current referral program: {paste the program rules,
the rewards, the trigger, the anti-sybil defenses}.

Here are my current numbers (last 30 days):
- Active users: {X}
- Invites sent: {Y}
- Invitees who signed up: {Z}
- Invitees who took primary action: {W}
- Total USDT paid in referral rewards: {$N}

Calculate:
1. Invites per active user, conversion rate, k-factor
2. CAC via referrals vs. my other channels
3. Cost as a % of revenue/LTV per invited user

Diagnose:
4. Where is the program leaking value? (Sybil attacks, over-rewarding,
   under-rewarding, friction at the share step, friction at the
   conversion step.)
5. The single change that would do the most to lift k-factor toward 1.0.
6. The single change that would do the most to reduce cost-per-referral
   without hurting growth.
```

## 9. Common pitfalls (from real Celo project autopsies)

- **The "$10 per referral" trap.** A founder excited about growth sets a too-generous payout. Sybil farmers descend within 48 hours. Treasury drained. Program shut down within a week. **Fix**: start small ($0.50-$2), scale up only after you've validated anti-sybil works.
- **Rewards that take forever to claim.** Users who refer a friend, see "your reward will be paid in the next batch" and never come back. **Fix**: pay instantly via the smart contract, or at minimum within 24 hours.
- **Asymmetric rewards (only inviter wins).** Conversion at the invitee step craters. **Fix**: always dual-sided — see §2.
- **Leaderboard that doesn't reset.** Whoever launched first dominates forever; newcomers stop trying within their first week. **Fix**: weekly or monthly seasons.
- **Referral codes nobody can remember.** "Use code A8X2-K9PL7 at signup" → users don't bother. **Fix**: human-readable codes (e.g. "MARIA42") or fully-link-based attribution with no code to type.
- **No referral content for the inviter to share.** Users want to refer but the "Share" button just gives them a URL. **Fix**: pre-write a WhatsApp/Telegram-ready message in the user's language, with the inviter's referral link embedded. See [growth-comms.md](growth-comms.md) for the share-message copy prompt.
- **Counting "shares" as "referrals."** A user who clicked "share" but generated zero signups is not a referral. **Fix**: only count converted invitees in any reward calculation or leaderboard.

## 10. The shareable artifact (don't skip this)

Every referral program needs ONE thing the user can paste into a chat without thinking. Build it as part of the program, not as an afterthought.

Minimum required:
- A short URL (`yourapp.com/r/MARIA42` — not the raw deep link)
- A pre-written message in the user's language ("Hey, I've been using {app} to save dollars — they're giving us both $2 if you sign up with my link 👉 ")
- An auto-generated image or short video (use Remotion — see [growth-gtm.md](growth-gtm.md) §4) that shows the app + the bonus + the inviter's username

Test it yourself: copy the link, paste into a WhatsApp test group, and ask if your message-recipient would tap it. If the answer is "I'd need to know more first," your shareable artifact is undercooked.

## 11. When NOT to add a referral program

Referrals aren't free engineering. Skip them if any of these are true:

- **You don't have product-market fit yet.** Referrals amplify whatever your product is. If it's not retaining users organically, referrals will bring in users who immediately churn — and you'll have paid for them.
- **Your LTV is too low to fund any meaningful reward.** If a user generates $0.10 in lifetime revenue, you can't afford a $1 referral reward. Build LTV first ([business-model.md](business-model.md)).
- **You haven't measured your baseline.** Without analytics ([growth-analytics.md](growth-analytics.md)) you can't tell if the referral program is working or just absorbing organic growth.
- **You don't have anti-sybil defenses.** Launching a referral program with no sybil protection on Celo is an invitation to lose your treasury within 48 hours. At minimum, implement the §6 starting stack before going live.

The right time to add referrals: you have ≥100 active users, you've seen organic referrals happen *without* an incentive program, your retention curve flattens (not zero), and you have a clear LTV number to budget against.
